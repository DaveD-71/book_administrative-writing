# Prompt & Task Writing Policy
## *Administrative Writing, Intermediate*

---

> **Purpose of this document**
> This policy defines standards for writing scenario prompts and task instructions across the intermediate book.
>
> It adapts the successful advanced prompt-control logic to the lower intermediate level rather than reusing advanced prompt density unchanged.

---

## 1. Primary Principle: The Task Must Stay Teachable At The Learner's Level

Every prompt and task should create a realistic opportunity to practise a specific writing skill without overloading the learner.

The scenario is the vehicle, not the point.

---

## 2. Intermediate Prompt Standards

Prompts should be:

- situation-anchored
- short and readable
- concrete rather than abstract
- explicit about audience and purpose
- level-appropriate in vocabulary and processing load

Prompts should not be:

- over-dense
- pseudo-realistic in unnecessary detail
- dependent on unexplained background knowledge
- advanced in sentence load just because the context is professional

---

## 3. Instruction Standards

Instructions should:

- tell the learner exactly what to write
- name the audience where relevant
- state the main goal of the text
- identify any required content points clearly
- avoid hiding multiple tasks inside one sentence

Guided tasks may be more explicit than in the advanced book.
Freer and homework tasks should still leave genuine writing work to the learner.

---

## 4. Option And Scenario Parallelism

When multiple options are offered:

- each option should require comparable writing work
- each option should be similar in specificity
- one option should not quietly contain more steps or more implied content than the others
- the options should feel like genuine alternatives, not superficial variants of the same scenario

---

## 5. Tone And Professional Realism

- Keep tone professional, polite, and ordinary.
- Workplace realism should feel recognizable, not highly specialized.
- Use sector-specific contexts selectively.
- Do not let realism become a pretext for overloaded instructions or niche jargon.

---

## 6. Intermediate Role And Audience Definition

Where a task depends on role or audience, define them functionally.

That means the learner should understand:

- who they are writing as
- who they are writing to
- why they are the person sending the message

Do not rely on bare job titles without communicative context.

---

## 7. Quality Checks For Prompt Review

When reviewing prompts, check:

1. Does the prompt stay readable for A2-B1+ workplace learners?
2. Does it give enough context without drowning the learner in background detail?
3. Is the audience clear?
4. Is the writing purpose clear?
5. Are required content points explicit?
6. Are options parallel where options exist?
7. Does the task keep real writing work with the learner rather than solving the writing for them?
