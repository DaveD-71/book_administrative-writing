# Intermediate Prompt & Task Writing Policy
## *Administrative Writing, Intermediate*

---

> **Purpose of this document**
> This policy defines standards for writing scenario prompts and task instructions across the intermediate book.
>
> Use it when drafting, revising, or evaluating prompts in the live module files. It is the source-of-truth prompt standard for the intermediate track.

---

## 1. Primary Principle: Learning Goals Drive Prompt Design

Every prompt exists to make the learner practise a specific, defined writing skill.

Before writing or revising a prompt, identify:

- the module-level skill band
- the unit-level writing goal
- the task-level behavior the learner must produce

A prompt is successful only if completing it well requires the learner to practise that target behavior.

Use this test:

*Could the learner complete this task without practising the intended skill?*

If the answer is yes, the prompt needs revision.

---

## 2. Intermediate Core Principle: Teachable Professional Realism

The intermediate book should feel like real workplace writing, but it should stay readable for A2-B1+ learners.

Prompts therefore need:

- a recognisable workplace situation
- a clear reason to write
- a visible audience or reader
- controlled context load
- instructions that are short, concrete, and explicit

Do **not** use realism as an excuse for heavy scenario background, dense wording, or unexplained institutional detail.

The learner should be challenged by the writing task, not by decoding the prompt.

Lower level does **not** justify a thinner teaching layer.  
The intermediate book must still explain why stronger writing choices work, not only show phrases or task formats.

---

## 3. The PAST-R Framework

Every meaningful writing task should make these elements recoverable:

| Element | Question | Intermediate standard |
|---|---|---|
| `P - Purpose` | Why is this document being written? | The communicative purpose should be explicit or easily inferable. |
| `A - Audience` | Who will read it? | Name the reader type clearly: supervisor, colleague, applicant, partner office, team. |
| `S - Situation` | What has happened? | Give a specific event or problem, not a general topic area. |
| `T - Type` | What kind of document is it? | Email, notice, summary, explanation, inquiry, short report, or similar. |
| `R - Role` | Who is the writer? | State or clearly imply why the learner is the person writing. |

Not every early guided task must state all five elements explicitly.  
But by Freer Practice, Extended Writing Task, and Homework, all five should be recoverable without guesswork.

---

## 3A. Explicit Teaching-Rationale Requirement

Meaningful writing tasks should be supported by short explicit explanation of **why** the stronger version works.

That explanation may appear in:

- model/example comparison
- language explanation
- guided support
- review / self-edit support

At minimum, meaningful units should explain one or more of the following:

- why the stronger version is easier for the reader to follow
- why the stronger version fits the audience better
- why the stronger version makes the task purpose clearer
- why the stronger version is more professional, neutral, or efficient

Do not rely on phrase exposure alone where the learner needs transferable decision logic.

---

## 4. Role And Audience Definition

Role definitions in the intermediate book should be:

- functional
- easy to understand
- tied to the reason for writing

Prefer:

- `You are replying to a staff member who needs the updated form.`
- `You are writing to your supervisor to explain the delay.`

Avoid:

- unexplained titles
- vague labels such as `administrative professional`
- roles that require background knowledge the learner does not have

Audience labels should also stay concrete:

- supervisor
- colleague
- applicant
- partner office
- meeting participants
- external contact

Do not leave the reader relationship vague when it affects tone or content choices.

When the reader relationship changes the wording significantly, include a short explanation of the effect of that audience change rather than leaving the learner to infer it alone.

---

## 5. Calibration By Activity Type

The amount of support should vary by activity type.

| Activity | Support level | Prompt standard |
|---|---|---|
| Controlled practice | High | Very short instruction; task focuses on one language or structure choice. |
| Guided Practice | High to medium | Scenario is bounded; some content, structure, or language support is provided. |
| Freer Practice | Medium | Situation is clear, but main content and organization decisions belong to the learner. |
| Extended Writing Task | Medium to low | Full document purpose is clear; learner must shape the response independently. |
| Homework | Low | Transfer-oriented task with minimal but sufficient framing. |

Do not let an intermediate Guided Practice become as open as an advanced freer task.

Support level should also control explanation density:

- Guided Practice and earlier layers should not only tell the learner what to include
- they should also tell the learner why the structure, tone choice, or information order helps

## 6. Scenario Choice Lists

Where a task offers options, use these rules.

### 6.1 Requirements belong at task level

State shared requirements once before the options:

- output type
- required content
- tone
- length or sentence-band guidance

Do not repeat those requirements inside each option unless an option truly differs for a pedagogical reason.

### 6.2 Keep options parallel

All options should be parallel in:

- length
- detail level
- difficulty
- output demand

No option should quietly pre-solve the task more than another.

### 6.3 Keep options meaningfully different

Options should represent genuinely different situations, not cosmetic rewrites of the same scenario.

Good variation may come from:

- different reader relationships
- different problem types
- different document purposes

---

## 7. Language And Style In Prompt Writing

### 7.1 Address the learner directly

Use direct second-person instructions.

Prefer:

- `Write an email to your supervisor.`
- `Complete the notice using the information below.`

Avoid textbook framing such as:

- `Students should write...`
- `Use the skills from this unit to...`

### 7.2 Use short, concrete wording

Prefer short, work-like verbs and nouns:

- write
- explain
- ask
- confirm
- update
- delay
- notice
- summary

Avoid abstract labels when a concrete situation can be named.

### 7.3 Keep lists tight

Required-content lists should include only information the learner genuinely needs.

Do not overload a lower-level learner with long bullet lists that restate what the document type already implies.

### 7.4 Keep tone professionally ordinary

The intermediate book should sound:

- professional
- polite
- clear
- ordinary

Do not write prompts in inflated bureaucratic language just to sound realistic.

---

## 8. JPO And Other Specialized Contexts

JPO or similar specialized context can be used where it clearly improves realism, but it must remain readable to non-specialist learners.

Rules:

- use specialized context sparingly
- explain only what the learner truly needs
- do not require specialist institutional knowledge to understand the task
- keep the writing skill transferable beyond the specific organization

The context may change, but the target writing behavior should remain generalisable.

---

## 9. Integrated Later-Book Tasks

Later units may involve:

- multiple audiences
- revision plus new writing
- a portfolio or capstone layer
- more than one document in a unit

When prompts become more integrated:

- keep each task's immediate purpose clear
- make the reader relationship explicit
- show how the task builds on earlier taught skills
- avoid stacking too many new demands in one instruction block

Later complexity is acceptable only when the unit still reads as an intermediate book unit, not an advanced book transplant.

---

## 10. Prompt Review Checklist

Before finalising a meaningful writing prompt, check the following:

- [ ] The prompt requires the intended unit skill rather than merely mentioning the topic
- [ ] The workplace situation is specific and recognisable
- [ ] Purpose, audience, situation, type, and role are recoverable at the right level for the task
- [ ] The wording is short, concrete, and readable for A2-B1+ learners
- [ ] The scenario does not overload the learner with unnecessary background detail
- [ ] The task or nearby support explains why the stronger writing choice works for the reader, purpose, tone, or document function
- [ ] Shared task requirements are stated once at task level, not repeated inside each option
- [ ] Choice-list options, where used, are parallel in length, detail level, and difficulty
- [ ] The task sounds professionally real without using inflated or opaque wording
- [ ] Later integrated tasks remain clearly staged and do not pile too many demands into one prompt
