# Learning Goals Mapping
## *Administrative Writing, Advanced* — Unit-by-Unit Analysis

---

> **Purpose of this document**
> This document maps the learning goals for all 23 units against three criteria:
> 1. **Fidelity** — do the goals match the original unit purpose?
> 2. **Level appropriateness** — do the goals suit B2–B2+ learners?
> 3. **Balance** — is there a good distribution of goal types across the book?
>
> Each unit entry lists the published learning goals, a fidelity and level note, and any flags for review. The balance analysis appears at the end.

---

## MODULE 1 — Advanced Foundations for Administrative Writing (Units 1–3)

---

### Unit 1 — Controlling Sentence Complexity

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify sources of ambiguity in long sentences
- restructure overly complex sentences using subordination, coordination, and segmentation
- apply clarity-focused patterns common in professional administrative writing
- analyse and revise authentic-style administrative text

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match the unit purpose precisely. The focus on subordination, coordination, and segmentation directly reflects the model text and language focus content.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate for B2–B2+. Sentence-level analysis and structural manipulation are core B2 tasks. The term "segmentation" and the demand to handle "authentic-style" texts add appropriate stretch.

**Flags:** None.

---

### Unit 2 — Tone for Agency-Level Communication

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify tone issues in professional correspondence
- revise direct or overly strong language into neutral, diplomatic phrasing
- apply softening strategies while maintaining clarity and authority
- write audience-appropriate messages for internal and external stakeholders

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well aligned. The phrase "maintaining clarity and authority" is particularly strong — it signals that diplomacy should not come at the cost of effectiveness, which is exactly what the unit teaches.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. Tone awareness at the interagency/international register is a genuine B2 challenge. The internal vs. external audience distinction adds appropriate complexity.

**Flags:** The goal "identify tone issues" is slightly lower-level than the others (more B1). Consider raising it to: *"diagnose and articulate tone issues in professional correspondence"* to better reflect the analytical demand.

---

### Unit 3 — Cohesion & Logical Flow

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify weaknesses in cohesion at sentence and paragraph level
- use cohesive devices appropriately and effectively
- apply thematic progression to improve clarity and readability
- reorganise disconnected content into a coherent paragraph
- write structured explanations with strong logical flow

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match well. "Thematic progression" is a precise, appropriate concept for this unit and is handled well in the language focus.

**Level:** ✅ Strong B2 level. Thematic progression in particular is upper-intermediate to advanced territory. The reorganisation task (D5) reflects the "reorganise disconnected content" goal directly.

**Flags:** Five goals is on the high side for a single unit. Consider whether "identify weaknesses" and "use cohesive devices" could be merged: *"identify and correct weaknesses in cohesion at sentence and paragraph level."*

---

## MODULE 2 — Professional Email Writing for Interagency Contexts (Units 4–7)

---

### Unit 4 — Structuring High-Level Emails

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- organise complex content using clear structural patterns
- adjust tone and formality for different audiences
- manage multi-part messages involving explanations, requests, and next steps
- write emails that balance clarity, diplomacy, and efficiency

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well matched. The phrase "balance clarity, diplomacy, and efficiency" captures the three-way tension the unit addresses through its model text and guided tasks.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. "Multi-part messages" and "balance" of competing demands are B2+ skills. The unit correctly positions email structure as a complex rhetorical task, not a formatting exercise.

**Flags:** The goal "adjust tone and formality for different audiences" is partially redundant with Unit 2. It is justified here because it's applied in the context of email structure rather than tone in isolation — but this overlap should be noted when reviewing balance.

---

### Unit 5 — Writing Precise Clarification Requests

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify vague or ambiguous requests and revise them for precision
- structure multi-part clarification inquiries logically
- reference documents, sections, and data accurately
- use diplomatic language to request missing, unclear, or contradictory information
- write concise but complete clarification messages suitable for high-level communication

**Fidelity:** ✅ Excellent alignment. "Reference documents, sections, and data accurately" is a distinctive and precise goal that is well reflected in the language focus and tasks.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. The combination of precision, diplomacy, and structural logic in a single message type is a demanding integration task.

**Flags:** Five goals again. "Identify vague requests and revise" and "write concise but complete messages" could be consolidated: *"write concise, precisely referenced clarification messages that avoid vagueness while maintaining diplomatic tone."*

---

### Unit 6 — Communicating Errors Diplomatically

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify language that sounds overly direct or assigns blame
- deliver corrective information without escalating tension
- explain the impact of errors clearly but professionally
- provide corrective guidance with tact and clarity
- use structured, neutral phrasing suitable for domestic and international partners

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well aligned. "Deliver corrective information without escalating tension" is a particularly strong goal — specific, contextualised, and measurable.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. The pragmatic complexity of this task (managing face-threatening acts in writing) is a genuine B2+ challenge that distinguishes this level from B1.

**Flags:** "Deliver corrective information without escalating tension" and "provide corrective guidance with tact and clarity" are near-synonymous. These could be merged or one reframed as the means (tact and clarity) and the other as the outcome (without escalating tension).

---

### Unit 7 — Layered Requests: Managing Multi-Part Actions

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- distinguish between primary and secondary requests
- structure multi-part requests to maximise clarity
- explain rationales and sequencing for each requested action
- use diplomatic and conditional phrasing to manage dependency steps
- write multi-step request emails that guide the reader through necessary actions

**Fidelity:** ✅ Strong alignment. "Manage dependency steps" is a precise and distinctive goal that clearly differentiates this unit from Unit 5 (clarification) and Unit 4 (general structure).

**Level:** ✅ B2+. The concept of dependency logic in professional writing is an advanced structural skill not typically encountered at B1.

**Flags:** "Structure multi-part requests to maximise clarity" and "write multi-step request emails that guide the reader" are overlapping. The first could be framed as the skill, the second as the application.

---

## MODULE 3 — Writing Complex Procedures & Internal Explanations (Units 8–11)

---

### Unit 8 — Writing Clear Procedures (Advanced)

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- organise procedural steps using coherent, logical sequencing
- identify missing, contradictory, or unclear procedural information
- distinguish between essential steps, conditions, and exceptions
- write procedures using consistent structure and formal administrative style
- revise procedural texts for clarity, accuracy, and usability

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match the unit well. The steps/conditions/exceptions distinction is a specific analytical skill that is taught explicitly and tested in both guided and freer writing tasks.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. Writing procedures with branching logic (conditions/exceptions) is a B2 task. The "revise for usability" goal adds a reader-focused dimension appropriate at this level.

**Flags:** None. This is one of the most coherent sets of goals in the book.

---

### Unit 9 — Change Management Writing

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- explain the rationale behind changes in procedure
- outline key differences between old and new processes
- identify risks, impacts, and necessary follow-up actions
- write change notifications suitable for internal, interagency, and international communication
- communicate changes diplomatically and accurately, avoiding ambiguity or unnecessary detail

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well aligned with a clear and coherent unit scope.

**Level:** ⚠️ Partial concern. "Identify risks, impacts, and necessary follow-up actions" overlaps significantly with Unit 13 (Risk & Impact Assessment). At this stage in the course (Module 3), risk identification may be premature as a goal — or it should be scoped more narrowly here (e.g., *"identify the likely impact of a procedural change on the reader or recipient"*) to distinguish it from the analytical risk assessment work in Unit 13.

**Flags:** The risk/impact goal needs narrowing to avoid conflation with Unit 13. Suggested revision: *"explain the practical implications of changes for the reader's workflow or responsibilities."*

---

### Unit 10 — Explaining Complex Issues

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- break down complex issues into clear components
- identify key factors, relationships, and impacts
- present issues logically using cause–effect, problem–solution, or multi-factor structures
- maintain a professional, neutral tone when describing difficulties or challenges
- write explanations appropriate for reports, inquiries, and procedural clarifications

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match well. The explicit naming of cause–effect, problem–solution, and multi-factor structures is precise and reflects the language focus content.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. The ability to select and deploy the appropriate explanatory structure (rather than just using one) is an advanced skill.

**Flags:** "Write explanations appropriate for reports, inquiries, and procedural clarifications" is quite broad and implies three different text types. Given that the unit's production tasks are paragraph-level explanations rather than full reports or inquiries, this goal could be scoped more tightly: *"write structured multi-factor explanations suitable for inclusion in reports, inquiries, or internal clarifications."*

---

### Unit 11 — Executive-Level Meeting Summaries

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify essential vs. non-essential meeting information
- combine paragraph and bullet-point structures for clarity
- summarise discussions objectively and diplomatically
- highlight decisions, action items, and outstanding issues
- write summaries that support follow-up tasks and demonstrate accountability

**Fidelity:** ✅ Strong alignment. "Demonstrate accountability" is a sophisticated goal that correctly captures the professional function of a meeting summary beyond mere information recording.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. The selectivity required (essential vs. non-essential) and the accountability framing are genuinely advanced professional skills.

**Flags:** None. One of the strongest goal sets in the book.

---

## MODULE 4 — Cross-Border & Interagency Communication (Units 12–15)

---

### Unit 12 — High-Level External Inquiries

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- write clear, well-structured inquiries requesting information or clarification
- identify and include essential background context
- formulate precise, answerable questions
- use diplomatic softening to avoid assigning blame or implying fault
- signal next steps and required timeframes professionally

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well matched. "Formulate precise, answerable questions" is a distinctive goal that differentiates this unit from the general clarification work in Unit 5.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. The external/international register raises the stakes compared to earlier email units, and the explicit requirement for "answerable questions" is a sophisticated pragmatic skill.

**Flags:** "Write clear, well-structured inquiries" is somewhat generic as a first goal — consider replacing with something that captures the external/diplomatic specificity: *"write formal external inquiries appropriate for international and interagency partner offices."*

---

### Unit 13 — Writing for Risk & Impact Assessment

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify different types of risks (procedural, operational, reputational, timeline-related)
- organise risk and impact explanations logically
- distinguish between factual description and evaluative language
- write clear risk statements, impact descriptions, and mitigation proposals
- communicate risk without exaggeration or emotional tone

**Fidelity:** ✅ Excellent alignment. The risk typology (procedural, operational, reputational, timeline-related) is specific and well reflected in the tasks.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. The ability to "distinguish between factual description and evaluative language" is a sophisticated metalinguistic awareness goal, and "communicate risk without exaggeration" requires precise hedging skills.

**Flags:** See Unit 9 note — the overlap between that unit's risk/impact goal and this unit's full risk assessment focus should be resolved at the Unit 9 end.

---

### Unit 14 — Writing Complex Multi-Audience Messages

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify the needs of internal, interagency, and international readers
- structure messages so information is accessible to all audiences
- avoid assumptions about prior knowledge
- balance detail and conciseness appropriately
- write layered explanations addressing multiple reader perspectives

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are precise and well differentiated from earlier audience-awareness goals in Unit 2. The focus here is structural (layered explanation, assumption avoidance) rather than tonal.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Writing a single text that simultaneously serves multiple audiences with different knowledge levels is an advanced rhetorical task.

**Flags:** "Identify the needs of internal, interagency, and international readers" is analytical; the remaining four goals are productive. This is a good balance for one unit. No significant concerns.

---

### Unit 15 — Drafting Policy-Aligned Rationales

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify the purpose of a rationale and distinguish it from a simple explanation
- align rationales with existing policies, guidelines, or organisational objectives
- structure rationales logically using policy reference, reasoning, and benefit statements
- write neutral, evidence-based rationale paragraphs
- calibrate the level of detail for different readers and purposes

**Fidelity:** ✅ Strong alignment. The distinction between a rationale and an explanation (first goal) is a genuine and important conceptual distinction that the unit teaches well.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Policy alignment, evidence-based reasoning, and audience calibration are all advanced professional writing skills.

**Flags:** "Calibrate the level of detail for different readers and purposes" is partially covered in Unit 14 (multi-audience) and Unit 20 (concise justifications). This goal is justified here because it applies specifically to the rationale context — but it should be checked that the unit tasks actually test this, rather than just stating it as a goal.

---

## MODULE 5 — Advanced Editing & Revision Skills (Units 16–18)

---

### Unit 16 — Writing Balanced Evaluations

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify strengths and weaknesses in a document or process
- write evaluations using neutral, evidence-based language
- structure evaluations using clear criteria
- avoid subjective, emotional, or overly negative tone
- provide constructive recommendations

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match well. "Structure evaluations using clear criteria" is precise and reflects the criterion-based language focus in Section C.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Writing a balanced evaluation requires metalinguistic awareness (distinguishing description from judgment) and pragmatic control (constructive framing of negatives) that are genuinely advanced.

**Flags:** None. Clean, well-scoped set of goals.

---

### Unit 17 — Drafting Executive Briefs

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify essential information for senior audiences
- structure briefs using standard components: overview, key points, implications, recommendation
- remove unnecessary detail
- write concise yet complete paragraphs
- present complex issues in a decision-ready format

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well matched. "Decision-ready format" is a strong, distinctive goal that captures the functional purpose of executive briefs.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Brevity at the expense of completeness is a common B2 failure mode; this unit directly targets it. The "decision-ready" framing adds an advanced professional dimension.

**Flags:** "Remove unnecessary detail" is stated as a goal but it describes a process rather than an outcome. Consider: *"select and prioritise information appropriate to the senior reader's decision-making needs."*

---

### Unit 18 — Synthesising Information from Multiple Sources

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify overlaps, differences, and contradictions across sources
- extract key points and organise them by theme
- write neutral, concise synthesis paragraphs
- use synthesis language to signal relationships between sources
- avoid listing information source-by-source

**Fidelity:** ✅ Excellent alignment. "Avoid listing information source-by-source" is a precise, teachable goal that directly reflects the most common error in synthesis writing — and the unit explicitly addresses this in both the model text and the editing tasks.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Source synthesis is widely considered an upper-intermediate to advanced skill. The explicit focus on thematic organisation (rather than sequential summary) is appropriately demanding.

**Flags:** None. One of the best-written goal sets in the book.

## MODULE 6 — Capstone & Applied Administrative Writing (Units 19–23)

---

### Unit 19 — Writing Policy Summaries for Non-Expert Audiences

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- identify which policy elements are essential for non-expert audiences
- simplify dense or technical policy language without losing accuracy
- write clear, structured explanations of policies, revisions, and requirements
- maintain neutrality and avoid over-interpretation
- highlight implications and actions plainly and professionally

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well aligned. "Simplify without losing accuracy" captures the central tension of plain-language writing precisely.

**Level:** ⚠️ Mild concern. This unit is placed in Module 6 (capstone), but the core skill — plain-language simplification — could arguably appear earlier. The goals feel slightly lower in cognitive demand than adjacent units (16–18). The addition of "avoid over-interpretation" and "maintain neutrality" elevates it, but this is worth reviewing in the context of overall module progression.

**Flags:** Consider whether the unit's placement in Module 6 is fully justified, or whether it could be repositioned in Module 4 or 5, with a more complex version of the task appearing in Module 6.

---

### Unit 20 — Writing Concise Justifications for Decisions

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- distinguish between a decision and a justification
- identify essential elements of a concise justification
- avoid unnecessary detail and emotional wording
- align justifications with policy, procedures, or evidence
- write short, clear paragraphs that support decision-making

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are appropriate. The decision/justification distinction (first goal) is a genuine conceptual clarification that the unit addresses well.

**Level:** ⚠️ Mild concern. Several of these goals overlap with Unit 15 (policy-aligned rationales): both units address policy alignment, evidence-based reasoning, and avoidance of vague language. The distinction — that Unit 15 focuses on longer, structured rationales while Unit 20 focuses on brief, embedded justifications — is real but may not be sufficiently clear from the goals alone.

**Flags:** The goals for Units 15 and 20 should be explicitly differentiated. Suggested addition to Unit 20: *"write justifications that are self-contained and appropriately brief for administrative approval contexts"* — framing brevity and self-containment as the distinctive demands of this unit type.

---

### Unit 21 — Drafting Consolidated Administrative Reports

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- consolidate multi-source information into a unified report
- structure reports using standard administrative components
- present issues, actions, and timelines clearly
- maintain professional, neutral tone throughout
- write reports that support decision-making and interagency coordination

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals match the unit well. The framing around decision-making support and interagency coordination is appropriate and consistent with the professional context.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Consolidating multi-source information into a structured report is a high-level integration task that draws on skills from Units 3, 10, 11, and 18.

**Flags:** "Maintain professional, neutral tone throughout" appears as a goal in multiple units (6, 13, 16, 18, 21). At this late stage in the course it should be assumed rather than listed, or framed as a higher-order goal: *"sustain appropriate register and neutrality across a multi-section document."*

---

### Unit 22 — Integrated Writing Task: Complex Scenario Response

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- interpret complex, incomplete, or conflicting information
- determine writing purpose and audience
- choose appropriate structure (email, brief, summary, report)
- combine multiple writing functions within one response
- demonstrate accuracy, clarity, and professional tone in extended writing

**Fidelity:** ✅ Goals are well matched to the unit's integrative function. "Interpret complex, incomplete, or conflicting information" is a distinctive and appropriately demanding goal for a pre-capstone unit.

**Level:** ✅ B2+. Structure selection and multi-function writing in a single response are genuine B2+ integration demands.

**Flags:** "Demonstrate accuracy, clarity, and professional tone in extended writing" is a meta-goal rather than a skill goal — it describes a quality standard rather than a learnable behaviour. Consider replacing with: *"integrate explanation, synthesis, and recommendation within a single, coherently structured document."*

---

### Unit 23 — Capstone: Full Administrative Writing Assessment

**Learning goals — learners will be able to:**
- synthesise information from multiple sources
- control tone and use diplomatic phrasing consistently
- select and apply appropriate structure
- maintain cohesion and thematic progression
- write clear explanations, requests, rationales, and recommendations
- adapt writing for different audiences

**Fidelity:** ✅ The goals function appropriately as a capstone checklist — they deliberately recapitulate the book's major skill strands rather than introducing new content.

**Level:** ✅ Appropriate. The combination of all six goals in a single extended task is the defining B2+ challenge of the assessment.

**Flags:** The goals read slightly like a course summary rather than unit-specific outcomes. This is appropriate for a capstone, but it may be worth adding one goal that is specific to the assessment context: *"demonstrate independent judgement in selecting, structuring, and producing an appropriate administrative document from multi-source input."*

## BALANCE ANALYSIS

### Goal Type Distribution across 23 units

The learning goals across the book can be categorised into five types. The count below reflects how many units list at least one goal of each type.

| Goal Type | Units | Count |
|---|---|---|
| **Structural / organisational** (sequencing, structuring, formatting output) | 1, 3, 4, 7, 8, 10, 11, 13, 14, 15, 17, 18, 21, 22 | 14 |
| **Linguistic / stylistic** (tone, register, phrasing, diplomatic language) | 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, 12, 13, 16, 18, 19, 22, 23 | 13 |
| **Analytical / critical** (identify, distinguish, evaluate, interpret) | 1, 2, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 22 | 14 |
| **Audience awareness** (adapt, calibrate, consider reader needs) | 2, 4, 9, 12, 14, 15, 19, 20, 22, 23 | 10 |
| **Productive / integrative** (write, produce, combine, synthesise) | All 23 units | 23 |

**Overall observation:** The balance is good. Structural, linguistic, and analytical goals are evenly distributed. Audience awareness is present in 10 units — proportionally lower, but appropriate given that it is a cross-cutting concern rather than a standalone skill in most units.

---

### Progression across modules

| Module | Primary goal emphasis |
|---|---|
| Module 1 (U1–3) | Sentence-level accuracy, cohesion, structural control |
| Module 2 (U4–7) | Email structure, tone, audience, diplomatic precision |
| Module 3 (U8–11) | Complex text types: procedures, explanations, summaries |
| Module 4 (U12–15) | Cross-border register, risk, multi-audience, rationale |
| Module 5 (U16–18) | Evaluation, synthesis, brevity, editing |
| Module 6 (U19–23) | Integration, assessment, independence |

**Observation:** The progression from sentence-level control (Module 1) through text-type mastery (Modules 2–4) to integrative skills (Modules 5–6) is coherent and well-designed. The shift from controlled to independent production is gradual and well-paced.
