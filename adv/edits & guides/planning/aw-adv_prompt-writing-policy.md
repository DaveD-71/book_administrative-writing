# Prompt & Task Writing Policy
## *Administrative Writing, Advanced*

---

> **Purpose of this document**
> This policy defines standards for writing scenario prompts and task instructions across all activity types in the textbook. It is a source of truth for the N7 unit editing phase and all subsequent revision work. Apply it when drafting new prompts, evaluating existing ones, and resolving inconsistencies between units.

---

## 1. Primary Principle: Learning Goals Drive Every Design Decision

Every prompt and task in this textbook exists to create an opportunity for the learner to practise a specific, defined skill. The scenario is the vehicle — not the point.

Before writing or evaluating any prompt, identify the relevant goals at three levels:

- **Module-level goal:** The broad communicative competence the module develops (e.g., cross-border and interagency communication)
- **Unit-level goal:** The specific writing skill the unit targets (e.g., drafting policy-aligned rationales)
- **Task-level goal:** The particular sub-skill the activity practises (e.g., structuring a rationale using policy reference → reasoning → benefit)

A prompt is well designed if — and only if — completing it **requires** the learner to exercise the target skill at the appropriate level. A scenario that is engaging, plausible, and professionally relevant but does not generate the target writing behaviour is a failed prompt, regardless of its other qualities.

**Test every prompt against this question:** *Could a learner complete this task well without practising the target skill?* If yes, the prompt needs revision.

---

## 2. Core Principle: Professional Verisimilitude

Prompts must feel like real work, not exercises. For adult professional learners, motivation comes from recognising a situation as one they have faced or will face — not from novelty or creativity for its own sake. A prompt that reads like a plausible work task generates investment. A prompt that reads like a textbook exercise does not.

**Every prompt should be anchored in:**
- a specific professional situation (not a topic or subject area)
- a clear communicative purpose (something that needs to be written and sent)
- a realistic recipient or audience
- consequences or stakes that a professional would recognise

**Weak (topic-anchored):**
> Write a risk and impact assessment describing a procedural or operational issue in an administrative context.

**Strong (situation-anchored):**
> A key partner agency has not yet completed training on a new document submission system, and the rollout deadline is two weeks away. Your supervisor has asked you to prepare a risk assessment for internal circulation before the project meeting on Thursday.

The strong version has a situation, a purpose, an audience, a timeframe, and implicit stakes. The learner is placed in a role and given a reason to write.

## 3. The PAST-R Framework

Every writing prompt — whether in Guided Writing (D), Freer Writing (E), or Homework (H) — should make the following elements identifiable, either explicitly or through clear inference:

| Element | Question | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **P — Purpose** | Why is this document being written? | Must be communicative, not academic. "To practise X" is not a purpose. |
| **A — Audience** | Who will read it? | Name the reader type: supervisor, partner office, interagency counterpart, etc. |
| **S — Subject** | What is the specific situation? | A defined event, problem, or decision — not a general topic area. |
| **T — Type** | What form does the output take? | Email, inquiry, rationale, risk assessment, notice, brief, etc. |
| **R — Role** | Who is the writer? | The learner's professional position in the scenario. Often implied in E1 and H3; must be explicitly stated in E3. See Section 3.1 for the E3 role-writing standard. |

Not all elements need to be spelled out in every prompt. In Guided Writing tasks, some elements are provided by the surrounding unit context. In Freer Writing and Homework, all five should be recoverable from the prompt itself.

## 3.1 E3 Role Definition Standard

In E3 (Role-Based Writing Task), the role is the defining feature of the activity. The role must be stated explicitly - it cannot be left to inference - and it must be written as a **functional description**, not a job title.

**A functional description tells the learner three things:**
1. What they do in their professional context
2. Who they write to (their relationship to the reader)
3. Why they are the appropriate person to write this document

**A job title alone tells the learner none of these things reliably.** Titles such as "liaison officer", "coordination officer", or "case coordinator" carry different meanings across different organisations. A learner who does not recognise the title cannot inhabit the role, which defeats the purpose of E3.

**Test:** Could a learner from any administrative workplace - regardless of their specific sector or organisation - understand this role and write from it convincingly? If not, the role needs rewriting.

**Weak (title only):**
> You are an external liaison officer in a regional review office.

A learner does not know from this what they do, who they report to, or who their counterpart is. "Regional review office" is also unexplained.

**Strong (functional description):**
> You handle document submissions on behalf of your office and are responsible for following up with partner agencies when materials go unacknowledged.

This works for any learner: the function is clear (handle submissions, follow up), the relationship to the reader is clear (partner agency), and the reason for writing is implicit in the situation.

**A second common error: the non-role**

Some E3 prompts assign a task rather than a role:

> You are writing from an interagency coordination team.
> You are responsible for preparing a consolidated report.

Neither of these is a role. The first gives an organisational location without a function. The second gives a task without a writer identity or a reader relationship. Both should be rewritten as functional descriptions before an E3 task is finalised.

**E3 role formats**

Roles in E3 tasks may be structured in one of two ways:

- **Single shared role + scenario choice:** One role is assigned at the task level; learners then choose from 2-4 situations that fit that role. This is the cleaner format and is preferred where the unit skill is best practised from one consistent perspective.
- **Per-option roles:** A different role is given in each scenario option. This is appropriate where the unit skill involves adapting to different professional relationships. Each option must still provide a functional description, not a title.

In either format, the role description belongs at the start of the task or option - before the situation is described - so the learner reads it in the right order: first who they are, then what has happened.

## 4. Calibration: The Right Level of Scenario Detail

Prompts must be calibrated so that the **scenario** is given, but the **analytical and writing work** remains entirely with the learner. Over-specification removes the cognitive demand the task is designed to create.

### 4.1 What the prompt should provide
- The situation: what has happened, what is pending, or what has been requested
- The writer's role and relationship to the reader
- The purpose: what the document needs to achieve
- Any constraints: word count, format requirements, required content elements

### 4.2 What the prompt should NOT provide
- Pre-identified risks, problems, findings, or conclusions that the learner is supposed to produce through the act of writing
- Excessive procedural detail that effectively drafts the content of the response

**Over-specified (removes analytical work):**
> The JPO is transitioning to a new international search report filing system. The new system is due to go live in six weeks. Compatibility testing with the USPTO's receiving platform has not yet been completed, and three of the JPO's regional examination teams have not yet received system training. A decision is needed on whether to proceed with the planned launch date or request a short extension.

This prompt has already identified the risks (compatibility gaps, training gaps) and framed the decision. The learner's core job — risk identification — has been done for them.

**Correctly calibrated:**
> The JPO is rolling out a new international search report filing system within six weeks, but preparation across partner offices and examination teams is incomplete. Write a risk assessment for internal circulation ahead of the project review meeting.

The situation is clear. The analytical work remains with the learner.

### 4.3 The calibration spectrum by activity type

| Activity | Scaffolding level | Scenario detail | Analytical demand on learner |
|---|---|---|---|
| D — Guided Writing | High | More detailed; may supply structure or template | Lower — applying a model or template |
| E — Freer Writing | Medium | Situation given; content decisions left to learner | Higher — independent organisation and content selection |
| H — Homework | Low | Minimal framing; learner selects content and structure | Highest — full independent production |

## 5. Scenario Choice Lists

Many E and H tasks offer learners a choice of scenarios. The following rules apply to all choice lists.

### 5.1 Separation of requirements and scenarios

Task requirements — word count, content elements, tone, structural expectations — are stated **once**, at the task level, before the scenario list. They apply equally to all options. Individual scenario items contain only the situational context; they do not repeat or vary the requirements.

**Incorrect (requirements duplicated inside options):**
> Choose one scenario and write a risk assessment.
>
> 1. A new procedural update is being rolled out across four regional offices. Include: 2–3 risks, causes and impacts, mitigation proposals, neutral tone.
> 2. *(JPO context)* The JPO is rolling out a new filing system but preparation is incomplete. Include: 2–3 risks, causes and impacts, mitigation proposals, neutral tone.

**Correct (requirements stated once; options contain context only):**
> Write a risk assessment. Include:
> - 2–3 clearly identified risks
> - causes and impacts for each
> - mitigation proposals
> - neutral, analytical tone
>
> Choose one context:
> 1. A new procedural update is being rolled out across four regional offices, but training has not yet been completed for all teams.
> 2. An interagency guideline has been revised but not yet distributed to all partner organisations.
> 3. A key staff member responsible for applicant communication has left without a formal handover.
> 4. *(JPO context)* The JPO is rolling out a new international filing system within six weeks, but preparation across partner offices and examination teams is incomplete.

### 5.2 Parallelism across all options

All options in a choice list must be parallel in:
- **Length** — one to two sentences each; no option should be substantially longer than others
- **Style** — all situation-anchored, not some topic-anchored and some situation-anchored
- **Detail level** — no option should pre-solve the task more than another
- **Scope** — all options should be equally achievable within the specified word count

Parallelism is essential because learners will make choices based on apparent difficulty and interest. A longer, more detailed option signals either that it is harder (discouraging) or that more of the work has been done for them (too easy). Neither is pedagogically sound.

### 5.3 The JPO context option

Where a JPO-specific scenario is included, it follows these rules:

- It is one option among equals — not a special case, supplement, or alternative framing of the task
- It is designed so that JPO learners recognise their professional context, but it is not so narrowly scoped that non-JPO learners cannot engage with it
- It is labelled *(JPO context)* immediately before the scenario text, using that exact label consistently across the book
- It matches all other options in length, detail level, and degree of pre-specification
- It does not overlap contextually with options 1–3 — it must represent a genuinely distinct situation, so that the total range of choice is not reduced
- A JPO student may choose any option, including 1–3; a non-JPO student may choose option 4 if the scenario interests them
- Most importantly: the JPO context option must require exactly the same target skill as all other options — only the scenario context differs, not the task

### 5.4 Avoiding contextual overlap between options

Options should represent meaningfully different professional situations — different document types, different professional relationships, or different problem types. Avoid listing variations of the same core scenario (e.g., three slightly different versions of "a process has changed and people haven't been told").

## 6. Language and Style in Prompt Writing

### 6.1 Address the learner directly and professionally
Prompts address the learner as a professional. Second person is standard.

**Avoid:** *Students should write a risk assessment that includes...*
**Use:** *Write a risk assessment that includes...*

### 6.2 Use concrete professional language
Vague category words — "administrative context", "a procedural issue", "a relevant situation" — signal that the scenario has not been committed to. Replace with specific professional events.

**Vague:** *Write about a procedural or operational issue in an administrative context.*
**Specific:** *A partner agency has missed the registration deadline for a new document submission portal. Write an internal risk note for your supervisor.*

### 6.3 Avoid academic framing
Prompts should not frame the writing task as a demonstration of learning.

**Academic framing (avoid):** *Use the skills from this unit to write a...*
**Professional framing (use):** *Write a...*

### 6.4 Keep required content lists tight
Bullet lists of required content elements should list only elements that are not already implied by the task type. A "formal external inquiry" does not need to be told to use "formal administrative tone" — that is already specified by the document type.

## 7. Examples in Task Instructions

Task instructions that describe a required output should include at least one brief inline example where there is any risk of ambiguity. This is particularly important for:
- named linguistic features (*diplomatic softening*, *hedging language*, *audience signals*)
- structural requirements (*cause–effect structure*, *layered explanation*)
- register descriptors (*neutral, analytical tone*)

A single inline example is usually sufficient:
> Use diplomatic softening throughout — for example, *"We would be grateful if you could confirm..."* rather than *"Please confirm..."*

## 8. Extension Tasks

Extension Tasks are optional activities that follow the Homework task. They are part of Section H, but always appear **after H3**, never before it. The correct ordering within Section H is:

1. **H2 — Real-World Task** (select units only, where assigned in the structural profile)
2. **H3 — Homework Writing Task** (present in every unit)
3. **Extension Task — Choose One** (always last)

Extension Tasks should be genuinely extensional — they deepen, transfer, or apply the unit skill beyond the textbook. They should not repeat the Homework task in a different form. Strong extension tasks involve:
- bringing in material from the learner's own workplace
- applying the unit skill to a different document type or context
- comparing the unit's model text against a real example
- creating a reusable resource (phrase bank, checklist, template)

---

## 9. Prompt Review Checklist

Before finalising any Freer Writing (E) or Homework (H) prompt, check the following:

- [ ] The prompt targets the module-, unit-, and task-level learning goals — completing it **requires** practising the target skill
- [ ] The prompt describes a specific situation, not a topic or category
- [ ] Purpose, audience, subject, type, and role (PAST-R) are all recoverable
- [ ] The scenario does not pre-identify content the learner is supposed to produce
- [ ] Task requirements are stated once at task level, not inside individual scenario options
- [ ] All options in a choice list are parallel in length, detail level, and scope
- [ ] The JPO context option (where present) is labelled *(JPO context)*, matches other options in length and detail, and does not overlap contextually with options 1–3
- [ ] Required content bullets list only non-obvious elements
- [ ] For E3 tasks: the role is written as a functional description (what the writer does and who they write to), not as a job title; a learner from any administrative workplace could understand and inhabit the role
- [ ] The prompt does not use academic framing
- [ ] Word count is specified and consistent with the module-level progression
- [ ] Extension Task appears after H3, not before it
