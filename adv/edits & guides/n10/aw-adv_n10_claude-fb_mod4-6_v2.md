## Issues that Stand After Profile Cross-Check

The following items from Version 1 are confirmed and retained. Where the profiles add context or nuance, that is noted.

### Cross-Cutting Issue: The "Writing goal" formula is insufficient

Across all three modules, N10 interventions frequently take the form: *"Writing goal: [task description]."* This is the central drift problem. A goal statement tells the learner what to aim for; it does not teach the principle that enables them to achieve it in a new situation. The N10 framework requires explanation of *what the feature is, why it matters in this context, and when to use it.* This is not met by a goal statement alone, even when a worked example follows.

This problem is most pervasive in Modules 4 and 5 and present in several Module 6 units.

## Module 4 — Cross-Border & Interagency Communication

### Unit 12 — High-Level External Inquiries

**Profile confirms:** A5 · B1 · B3 · C2 · C8 · D1 · D4 · E3 · F1/F2/F3 · G2 · H1 · H2 · H3.
Note: This is the only unit with H1 + H2 + H3. Profile flags this as unusually heavy and questions whether H1 and H2 are sufficiently distinct. H1 is correctly present in the N10 file. ✅

**What's working:**
B1 "Why this works" note is specific and correctly identifies the structural principle (background → focused questions → timeline need). C8 worked mini-inquiry has useful accompanying commentary. D4 transfer reminder is well targeted.

**N10 issues:**

**C1 — "Providing Background Context" has no conceptual framing; only a goal statement.**
The section opens with *"Writing goal: Give the reader enough information to locate the issue and understand what you are asking about."* This is a task description. The principle — why background context is especially necessary in external communication where the reader cannot draw on shared case knowledge, and how writers decide what level of context is appropriate — is never explained. B3 actually contains the relevant principle (*"External inquiries usually need more explicit reader orientation than internal messages"*) but this is not referenced in C1. The C1 framing should either be rewritten to include this principle, or explicitly point back to B3.

**C2 — "Asking Clear, Answerable Questions" has the same problem.**
Goal statement, example, phrase list — but no explanation of the principle. The contrast between a focused question and a broad topic prompt is mentioned (*"Broad topic prompts force the reader to guess"*) but not explained. The C8 phrase bank also lacks any framing about why these phrases work; the worked mini-inquiry does useful explanatory work but comes after the phrase bank rather than before it (sequencing: framing → worked example → phrase bank would be more effective).

**Verdict:** Light N10 cleanup needed in C1 and C2. Sequencing of C8 worth reviewing.

### Unit 13 — Writing for Risk & Impact Assessment

**Profile confirms:** A4 · B1 · B7 · C3 · C4 · D1 · D2 · E1 · F1/F3 · G4 · H1 · H3.
Note: G4 (Grammar & Accuracy Focus) is the only use of this code in the book — profile flags it as narrow but appropriate.

**What's working:**
B1 "Why this works" note is the strongest in Module 4 — names four distinct functions and explains that weak risk writing blurs them. B7 framing (*"Risk language should come from evidence, not from alarm"*) is a genuine principle. C4 worked example is effective. G4 hedging table is a strong structural addition.

**N10 issues:**

**C1 — The factual/risk contrast principle is buried in C3 instead of C1.**
The opening C1 framing is better than most: *"Risk writing is evidence-based and conditional. The writer is not claiming that a problem has already fully occurred; the writer is identifying what may happen, why it may happen, and how strongly the available information supports that concern."* But the key distinction — that factual status statements describe what is happening now, while risk statements project what may happen — appears only in the C3 task instruction (*"Factual status statements describe what is happening now. Risk statements project what may happen if the current condition continues. Strong risk writing keeps those two functions distinct"*). This is the central principle of the unit. It belongs in C1, not buried in a task instruction at C3.

**C2 — "Explaining Impact and Proposing Mitigation" has only a goal statement as framing.**
*"Writing goal: Explain the consequence of a risk and propose a realistic step to reduce it."* No explanation of what a mitigation measure is (a realistic, proportionate response to a specific risk, not a general intention), or why separating impact from mitigation matters. The worked example carries all the explanatory weight the framing section should be doing.

**Verdict:** Light cleanup needed. Main fix is moving the factual/risk contrast from C3 into C1, and adding genuine framing to C2.

### Unit 14 — Writing Complex Multi-Audience Messages

**Profile confirms:** A6 · B1 · B4 · C3 · C5 · D1 · D4 · E4 · F1/F3 · G2 · H1 · H3.
Note: E4 is the ★ first use of Multi-Part Portfolio Task (introduced here). Profile confirms E4 word counts (80–100 internal + 160–200 external = 240–300 combined) are within module range. ✅

**What's working:**
B4 annotated model is the strongest activity in Module 4 — each annotation names a technique and explains what it achieves, and the "Layered explanation" annotation makes the unit's core principle explicit. C5 worked example is well constructed. D4 transfer reminder correctly identifies what stays constant vs. what changes.

**N10 issues:**

**C1 — "Audience Signals and Layered Explanation" has only a goal statement.**
*"Writing goal: Address different readers explicitly and provide information appropriate to each audience."* The principle — that an audience signal explicitly separates the reading experience for different groups, so specialist readers are not burdened with redundant context and non-specialist readers are not left without the context they need — is not stated. **C2 — "Avoiding Assumed Knowledge" is better but still incomplete.**

But the harder skill — how writers *identify* assumed knowledge in their own drafts — is not taught. What is the cognitive move that lets a writer notice when they have built in a dependency on shared context?

**Verdict:** Needs cleanup in C1 and C2. Both need genuine principle-level framing rather than goal statements.

### Unit 15 — Drafting Policy-Aligned Rationales

**Profile confirms:** A5 · B1 · B6 · C3 · C7 · D1 · D3 · G3 · H1 · H3.
Note: B6 (★ first use here) and G3 (★ first use here) are both correctly present. Profile confirms B6 is well chosen and well integrated. Profile also flags E1 word count (180–220) as below module target and below H3 range (220–260) — worth noting, though this is a P5 check, not N10.

**What's working:**
B1 "Why this works" note names the three-element chain (policy requirement → operational problem → benefit) clearly. C7 worked example is explicit about what it demonstrates. D3 transfer reminder (*"Use the template as a logic chain rather than as fixed wording"*) is the best in Module 4.

The B6 framing is strong: *"Policy references only strengthen a rationale when they are linked directly to the current decision. Simply naming a guideline is not enough; the writer has to show what the requirement means for the recommendation being proposed."* This is exactly the principle that C1 needs.

**N10 issues:**

**C1 — "Referencing Policy or Guidelines" has only a goal statement, and the needed principle is in B6.**
*"Writing goal: Ground the rationale in existing rules, requirements, or organisational objectives."* The key principle — distinguishing citing a policy from using it — is in B6 but not in C1. Either C1 should be rewritten to include this principle, or C1 should explicitly reference the B6 framing.

**C2 — "Building the Rationale: Reasoning and Benefits" has only a goal statement.**
*"Writing goal: Provide objective reasons grounded in process needs, and explain how the change improves outcomes."* The distinction between rationale (why this decision follows from a problem or policy) and benefit (what improvement follows) is not explained. These are related but functionally distinct, and conflating them is a common learner error.

**Verdict:** Light cleanup needed in C1 (cross-reference or import the B6 principle) and C2 (distinguish rationale from benefit explicitly).

### Unit 16 — Writing Balanced Evaluations

**Profile confirms:** A3 · B1 · B4 · C3 · C4 · D1 · D6 · E1 · F2/F3 · G3 · H1 · H3.
Note: D6 (★ first use of Revision Chain, introduced here) is correctly present. Profile flags that the unit goal ("with appropriate hedging and evidence") doesn't align well with the unit content, which focuses on criterion-based evaluation language — this is a learning-goal issue, not N10. D6 references learners' Unit 15 writing; profile flags robustness concern in self-study contexts but confirms a fallback text is already provided. ✅

**What's working:**
B4 annotation (f) about cautious hedging language (*"generally", "may"*) is a genuine teaching point. C4 worked example is effective.

**N10 issues:**

**B1 "Why this works" note is too thin.**
*"The improved evaluation is more credible because it separates observation from judgement. It names specific strengths and weaknesses, supports them with evidence, and frames recommendations as constructive improvements rather than personal criticism."* This is correct but doesn't explain *why* the observation/judgement distinction matters rhetorically — i.e., that evaluation language sounding like personal opinion undermines the writer's credibility, while evidence-based language positions the evaluation as professional and defensible. The principle behind the distinction is stated but not explained.

**C3 framing introduces "criterion-based" without defining it.**
*"Objective evaluation language is evidence-based, criterion-based, and proportionate."* The term "criterion-based" appears without definition. The phrase "in terms of [criterion]" appears in the expression list but what a criterion *is* in evaluation writing is never explained. Learners who don't already understand this concept won't learn it here.

**C4 framing introduces overclaiming but doesn't explain calibration.**
The framing correctly identifies the problem (*"Strong evaluation writing makes room for partial success, localised weaknesses, and improvement needs"*) but provides no guidance on *how* to calibrate — using hedging language, localising scope, or distinguishing significant from minor weaknesses.

**No transfer cue between C and D1.** D6 has a transfer reminder, but there is no bridge connecting C section principles to D1 (Model Rewriting), the first production task.

**Verdict:** Needs cleanup. "Why this works" note, C3 "criterion-based" gap, and C4 calibration gap all need attention.

### Unit 17 — Drafting Executive Briefs

**Profile confirms:** A4 · B1 · B7 · C3 · C5 · D1 · D7 · E1 · F1/F3 · G2 · H1 · H3.
Note: Profile flags the unit goal ("from complex multi-source material") as misaligned — Unit 17 doesn't consistently use multi-source inputs; that's Unit 18's territory. Suggested revision: "Draft concise, structured executive briefs that present complex issues in a decision-ready format." This is a learning-goal issue, not N10.

**What's working:**
B7 framing is the strongest conceptual framing in Module 5 — explains what an overview sentence does and why it must precede the details. C5 worked example correctly shows the transformation from meeting-room language to executive register. D7 transfer reminder is the best in the module.

**N10 issues:**

**B1 "Why this works" note misses the selection principle.**
*"The improved brief foregrounds decision-relevant content. Instead of retelling the discussion, it selects the points an executive needs in order to understand the issue, its implications, and the recommended next step."* This is correct but doesn't explain the principle behind the selection: that executive readers need information grouped by decision-relevance (what the problem is, what it implies, what should be done), not by the order events occurred. The principle of decision-oriented selection is never taught explicitly.

**C3 framing is another goal statement.**
*"Writing goal: Provide immediate context and distil information to essentials."* This describes what an overview does, not how to write one or why it needs to work differently from a detail list. The B7 framing actually explains overview function better than C3 — which is the wrong direction. The conceptual explanation belongs in C, not only in B.

**"Implications" receives no C-section language instruction.**
The four-part structure (Overview / Key Points / Implications / Recommendation) is introduced in B1 and practised throughout. But there is no C-section framing for what an implication is, how it differs from a key point (it projects forward rather than describing what happened), or what language is appropriate for it. The H1 extension task explicitly acknowledges that Implications is typically the weakest section of a brief — but the gap in C means this weakness is not addressed by instruction.

**Verdict:** Needs cleanup. C3 goal-statement problem, missing Implications framing, and the B1 selection-principle gap all need attention.

### Unit 20 — Writing Concise Justifications for Decisions

**Profile confirms:** A2 · B1 · C3 (×3) · C6 · D1 · D2 · E1 · F1/F3 · G2 · H3 only — no H1 or H2. ✅ per profile.

### Unit 21 — Drafting Consolidated Administrative Reports
