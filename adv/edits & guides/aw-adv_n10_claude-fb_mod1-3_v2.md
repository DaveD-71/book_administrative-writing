This is the most significant new finding. The structural profiles specify H3 only (no H1) for Units 2, 3, 4, and 11. The N10 files contain H1 Extension Tasks in all four of these units. The profiles also specify H2 + H3 (no H1) for Unit 6; the N10 file contains H2 + H3 + H1.

Units where H1 is confirmed correct per profiles: U1 ✅, U5 ✅, U7 ✅, U8 ✅, U9 ✅, U10 ✅.

**What to do about this:** These H1 tasks are present in the N10 files but absent from the profiles. There are two possible explanations: either the H1 tasks were added during the N9 pass (which required H1 in every unit) and the profiles were not updated to reflect exceptions that were later decided; or the profiles are correct and these H1s should be removed. This needs a decision from Dave before action is taken — the H1 tasks themselves are well-written and appropriate, so the question is whether the profile design decision (H3-only for these units) is still the intended one, or whether it should be updated.

This is not an N10 issue per se, but it should be resolved before N11 QA.

### F section duplication in Units 2 and 3

The profiles specify a single F1 (Guided Peer Review) + F3 (Self-Editing Checklist) for both Unit 2 and Unit 3. The N10 files contain **two F1 headings** in each unit: a loose "Peer Review (F1)" block followed by a separate "Guided Peer Review (F1)" block, then F3.

Unit 1 does not have this problem — it has a single "Peer Review (F1)" block followed by F3, which matches the profile assignment.

The duplication in Units 2 and 3 creates a structural anomaly: two sets of peer review questions under two differently labelled headings for the same activity code. One of the two F1 blocks in each unit should be removed. The "Guided Peer Review (F1)" version in each case has the more specific, criterion-based questions aligned with the unit's learning goals — that is the one to keep. The loose "Peer Review (F1)" opener is the one to remove.

This was flagged in the previous session summary as a Unit 3 issue only. It is actually present in both Units 2 and 3.

## Corrections to Previous Session Summary

The following claims from the prior session summary about Modules 1–3 were wrong or imprecise:

**"Unit 3 C2 thin framing on reference vs. repetition."**
After reading the file: Unit 3 C section sub-section 2 ("Reference and Continuity") reads: *"Reference improves flow when the reader can still identify the meaning clearly. If the reference word becomes vague, repetition is better. Good writers choose between repetition and reference based on what gives the reader the clearest path through the paragraph."* This is a genuine conceptual framing — it explains the decision principle, not just the task. The previous flag was wrong. This framing is adequate. **Withdrawn.**

**"Unit 9 'three communicative functions' principle appears before C3, not C7."**
This was confused in the summary. The principle appears at the opening of C3 ("Change messages usually combine three communicative functions: state the change, explain its impact or rationale, and reassure the reader about what to do next"). That is exactly where it should be — as the framing for C3 before the practice items. C7 (Expansion Task) follows C3 in the same section. The principle is correctly positioned. **Withdrawn.**

**"Unit 10 C2/C3 framing overlap."**
After reading the file: C2 framing ("Accessible explanation is not the same as making the issue simpler than it really is. The writer's job is to control information load by breaking the issue into clear parts, naming each part explicitly, and showing how the parts affect the next stage of work.") and C3 framing ("Simplification should make the message clearer, not thinner. A strong explanation replaces vague statements with specific information and keeps the necessary meaning, instead of removing the detail the reader needs in order to understand the issue.") are thematically related but functionally distinct. C2 is about structure and information control; C3 is about specificity and the risk of over-simplification. These address different learner problems and do not overlap in any problematic way. **Withdrawn.**

**"Unit 3 duplicate F sections" attributed to Unit 3 only.**
Partially correct, but the same pattern also appears in Unit 2. Corrected above.

## What Actually Stands: N10 Quality Assessment, Modules 1–3

Having read the full text of all three module files, the following assessment replaces the summary-level claims from the previous session.

### Module 1 — Advanced Foundations for Administrative Writing

#### Unit 1 — Controlling Sentence Complexity

**What's working:**
The C section opens with a genuine key principle: *"Sentence clarity is not primarily about length — it is about logical load."* This is the central conceptual statement of the unit and it correctly frames everything that follows. The three pattern sub-sections (Coordination, Subordination, Segmentation) each have framing that explains function before presenting phrases. The note on the coordination/subordination contrast (the *but* vs. *although* comparison) is the strongest teaching moment in Module 1 — it explains a genuinely difficult distinction that learners at B2 often conflate. The Patterns 1–2 comparison note explains the difference in order and emphasis clearly. Pattern 3 has a worked example. B1 "Why this works" is correct and specific.

**N10 issues:**

The principle that *"clarity is about managing logical load, not just reducing length"* is stated once at the top of C but not reinforced at the point where learners are most likely to need it: D2 (Write Your Own Complex Sentence). The D2 "Before you write" note says *"Do not choose the longest sentence as the 'best' version automatically"* — which is the right nudge, but it names the misconception rather than explaining the underlying principle. A brief restatement of the logical-load principle at D2 would complete the transfer arc.

The → symbol used in the pattern titles is explained once (at the top of C3). ✅

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. One small transfer-cue improvement possible at D2.

#### Unit 2 — Tone for Agency-Level Communication

**What's working:**
The C1 (Softening Strategies) framing is strong: *"Softening does not mean being vague or weak. It means presenting a problem in a way that keeps the reader cooperative, especially when the message could otherwise sound accusatory, impatient, or embarrassing."* This explains the principle and addresses the most common learner misconception. The C2 (Neutral Clarification Language) framing is also purposive: *"Neutral clarification language is useful when you need accurate information but do not want to imply fault before the facts are confirmed."* The C3 (Audience-Appropriate Tone) worked example (internal / interagency / foreign office register gradient) is clear and practical. The B1 "Why this works" note is correct. The D4 transfer reminder is well targeted.

**N10 issue:**

The C3 (Audience-Appropriate Tone) section explains the gradient clearly through example but does not state the underlying principle: that the amount of softening and explicitness a writer uses should be calibrated to the relational distance and formality of the audience, because the cost of a perceived slight increases as the relationship becomes more formal or distant. The worked example demonstrates this but the principle behind it — *why* more distance requires more softening — is not stated. This is a light gap.

**Structural issue (not N10):**
F section has two F1 headings (see above). The loose "Peer Review (F1)" opener should be removed; the "Guided Peer Review (F1)" block should be retained.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. Light gap in C3 principle explanation. F section structural fix needed.

#### Unit 3 — Cohesion & Logical Flow

**What's working:**
The C section opens with a genuinely conceptual statement on cohesive devices: *"Cohesive devices do more than 'make writing smoother'. They tell the reader exactly how one idea relates to the next: addition, result, contrast, or specification."* This is a correct and useful principle. The C2 (Reference and Continuity) framing — the decision between reference words and repetition based on reader clarity — is a sound teaching point (and was incorrectly flagged as thin in V1; it is adequate). The C3 (Thematic Progression) worked example is clear and the B1 "Why this works" note is correct.

**N10 issue:**

The C section has three distinct sub-sections (Cohesive Devices / Reference and Continuity / Thematic Progression) but no overarching framing that tells learners how the three connect — i.e., that cohesion at the sentence level (connectors), at the phrase level (reference words), and at the paragraph level (thematic progression) are three different but complementary dimensions of the same skill. The learner encounters them as a list rather than as an integrated system. A brief orientation sentence at the top of C would resolve this.

**Structural issues (not N10):**
- F section has two F1 headings. Same fix as Unit 2: remove the loose "Peer Review (F1)" opener, retain "Guided Peer Review (F1)".
- H section contains an H1 Extension Task that is not in the profile (profile specifies H3 only). See note above on H1 discrepancies.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. Light gap in C section orientation. Structural fixes needed.

### Module 2 — Professional Email Writing for Interagency Contexts

#### Unit 4 — Structuring High-Level Emails

**What's working:**
The C section framing is the strongest in Module 2: *"A strong professional email is organised around reader needs, not simply around the order in which the writer thought of the points."* This is a genuine principle — not a task description, and not just a structural rule. The six-part sequence is presented with useful phrases and a worked example that explicitly annotates each sentence's function. The B3 (Structural Deconstruction) framing note is also conceptually grounded: *"Each section supports the reader in a different way: orienting them, explaining why the message matters, stating what action is needed, and making clear what will happen next."* The D3 transfer reminder correctly identifies the template as a logic chain, not a formula.

**N10 issue:**

The B1 "Why this works" note is functional but thin compared to the C section quality: *"The improved version helps the reader act efficiently because each part of the email has a clear job."* This is correct but descriptive. A stronger note would explain *why* structural separation of context, explanation, and request reduces reader effort — i.e., that readers in busy professional contexts scan for what they need to do, and mixing explanation with request forces them to re-read to disentangle them. The principle behind the format is left implicit.

**Structural issue (not N10):**
H section contains an H1 Extension Task not in the profile (profile specifies H3 only). See note above.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. B1 "Why this works" note could be strengthened. Structural fix needed.

#### Unit 5 — Writing Precise Clarification Requests

**What's working:**
The C1 framing is conceptually sound: *"Precision in clarification writing means reducing the reader's interpretive work. A strong clarification request identifies the exact point in question, limits the scope of each query, and makes clear why the clarification is needed."* This is a principle, not a task description. The C8 (Phrase Bank Building) worked example explicitly explains what makes it effective: *"each question is tied to a specific reference, each point asks for one answerable clarification."* The B1 "Why this works" note correctly identifies answerability as the key criterion: *"The improved version is easier to answer because each question is anchored to a specific place in the document and asks for one clear piece of clarification."* The D4 transfer reminder is well targeted (precision stays constant; what changes is diplomatic framing).

**N10 issue:**

The C section has two sub-sections (Useful Phrases / Structuring Multi-Part Questions) but the transition between them is slightly abrupt. The principle in C1 (reduce the reader's interpretive work) is not explicitly carried forward into C2. The C2 framing (*"A useful sequence moves from context, to clearly separated points, to the action your office will take once the reply is received"*) is structural rather than principled — it describes what to do but not why this sequence specifically reduces interpretive work for the reader. A brief bridging sentence would link the C1 principle to the C2 structural guidance.

The "Role reminder" note in E (before the freer writing task) is an unusual placement — it interrupts the task instruction rather than appearing as an introductory note before it. This is a minor formatting issue but slightly confusing in layout.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. Light gap in C1–C2 bridging. No major N10 issues.

#### Unit 6 — Communicating Errors Diplomatically

**What's working:**
This is the strongest N10 unit in Module 2. The C1 (Diplomatic Frames) framing is fully conceptual: *"In diplomatic error notification, the writer's job is to make the issue visible without making the reader defensive. This usually means depersonalising the problem, signalling uncertainty where appropriate, and shifting attention toward correction and next steps."* This explains the principle, the mechanism, and the three techniques in a single framing statement. The C2 (Explaining Impact without Assigning Blame) framing is also principled: *"A neutral impact statement explains why the correction matters without presenting the reader as the cause of the problem."* The C4 worked example is the clearest in Module 2 — it contrasts direct and diplomatic versions and explicitly names what changed and why. The B4 annotated model is excellent: each annotation explains a specific writing decision with a reason, not just a label.

**One minor N10 gap:**
The B4 annotation for the final "Once clarified, we will be able to proceed…" sentence correctly identifies it as a next-step statement but describes it as showing "the writer is ready to act." The deeper principle — that a next-step statement shifts the communicative focus from fault to resolution, and signals collaborative intent — is gestured at but not fully stated. This is a very light gap.

**Structural issue (not N10):**
H section contains H1 in addition to H2 + H3. Profile specifies H2 + H3 only; H1 is not in the profile. See note above.

**Verdict:** Passes N10 with minor gap in one B4 annotation. Structural fix needed.

#### Unit 7 — Layered Requests: Managing Multi-Part Actions

**What's working:**
The C1 (Sequencing and Prioritising Requests) framing is principled: *"When a message contains several requests, order is part of the meaning. The sequence tells the reader what matters most, what can wait, and which actions depend on earlier confirmation."* This explains why sequencing matters, not just how to do it. The C5 (Paragraph-Level Transformation) worked example explicitly states what makes the sequenced version better: *"The second version is easier to follow because the order, dependency, and reason for the sequence are made explicit."* The B1 "Why this works" note correctly identifies explicit priority and dependency as the key differences. The D2 transfer reminder is the best in Module 2: *"Do not just add sequence markers mechanically. Use them to show the reader which action unlocks the next stage and why that order matters."*

**N10 issue:**

The C section has only one sub-section and one phrase list before going straight to Practice A (C3) and then C5. There is no framing for the C5 (Paragraph-Level Transformation) task specifically — the worked example is present and well annotated, but there is no statement of the principle that transformation tasks require before the example. The C5 example and its explanation do the teaching work, but a one-sentence framing before the worked example would make the structure more consistent with the rest of the book.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. Very light gap in C5 framing.

### Module 3 — Writing Complex Procedures & Internal Explanations

#### Unit 8 — Writing Clear Procedures (Advanced)

**What's working:**
The C section is well structured. C6 (Sequencing/Ordering Task) opens with a genuine principle: *"In procedural writing, order is part of the meaning. Readers need a sequence they can follow without guessing which action starts the process, which actions depend on earlier checks, and which step closes the task."* The C3 (Sentence Rewriting) framing is also genuinely conceptual: *"Procedural instructions are not the same as general explanation. A procedure tells the reader exactly what action to perform, using precise verbs and a stable instruction style, rather than broad descriptive language."* The worked example correctly names what changed and why. The B1 "Why this works" note is specific and accurate.

**N10 issue:**

The C section puts C6 (the sequencing/ordering task) *before* C3 (sentence rewriting with precise verbs). This means learners practise sequencing before they have been taught the precise verb vocabulary that makes procedural steps clear. The sequencing principle (C6) and the instruction-language principle (C3) are related but functionally distinct; the current order may be slightly counterintuitive pedagogically. This is a sequencing note rather than a missing-principle issue, and it does not constitute a major N10 gap — the principles are both present and well stated.

There is no transfer cue between C and D1. The D5 task has a good transfer reminder; D1 does not.

**Verdict:** Passes N10. Minor C-section sequencing note and missing D1 transfer cue.

#### Unit 9 — Change Management Writing

**What's working:**
The C3 (Sentence Rewriting) framing is strong and correctly positioned: *"Change messages usually combine three communicative functions: state the change, explain its impact or rationale, and reassure the reader about what to do next. If one of these functions is missing, the message often feels abrupt, incomplete, or difficult to act on."* This is a principle that explains the *why*, and it appears at the opening of C as the framing for what follows. (This was incorrectly flagged in the prior session summary as misplaced — it is correctly positioned in C3, not in C7.) The C7 (Expansion Task) worked example is clear and the explanation after it correctly names the three communicative functions that the expanded version adds. The B1 "Why this works" note and B4 annotations are both specific and well constructed. The D3 transfer reminder is the best in Module 3: *"Do not treat the template as five isolated boxes. Use it to preserve the logic of effective change communication."*

**N10 issue:**

The C3 framing names three functions but does not explain why *all three* are necessary together — i.e., why stating the change without rationale produces resistance, why rationale without instruction produces confusion, and why instruction without reassurance can produce anxiety. The principle is stated as a list of three functions; the interdependency between them is implicit. A sentence explaining why the combination matters (rather than just listing the parts) would complete the framing.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. One light gap in explaining the three-function interdependency.

#### Unit 10 — Explaining Complex Issues

**What's working:**
The C2 (Classification/Matching) framing is conceptually sound: *"Accessible explanation is not the same as making the issue simpler than it really is. The writer's job is to control information load by breaking the issue into clear parts, naming each part explicitly, and showing how the parts affect the next stage of work."* The C3 (Sentence Rewriting) framing is also distinct and useful: *"Simplification should make the message clearer, not thinner. A strong explanation replaces vague statements with specific information and keeps the necessary meaning, instead of removing the detail the reader needs in order to understand the issue."* These two framings address different learner risks (structural vagueness vs. false simplification) and do not overlap. (The prior session summary flagged overlap — this was incorrect.)

The C3 worked example is the strongest in Module 3: it shows the transformation from vague to specific and names precisely what changed and why.

The B1 "Why this works" note is accurate and focused on what matters for this unit: *"it names the number of factors up front, separates them clearly, and supports each one with specific detail."*

**No significant N10 issues.** This unit is the most consistently N10-complete unit in Module 3.

**Verdict:** Passes N10. ✅

#### Unit 11 — Executive-Level Meeting Summaries

**What's working:**
The B7 (Data Interpretation Input) framing is strong and purposive: *"Executive summaries convert raw meeting or status data into management-ready information. That means writers do not simply repeat numbers: they decide what the data shows about outcomes, outstanding items, responsibilities, and follow-up needs."* This explains the principle behind the activity, not just the task. The B1 "Why this works" note is the best in Module 3: *"The improved version does not retell the meeting in time order. Instead, it groups information by managerial function: what the meeting was about, what mattered, what was decided, and what happens next."* This is exactly the kind of principle-level teaching the framework requires.

The C3 (Sentence Rewriting) framing is also principled: *"Executive-summary language is action-focused. Instead of narrating who said what during the meeting, it highlights decisions, responsibilities, unresolved items, and next steps in a neutral, concise form."*

**N10 issue:**

The C5 (Paragraph-Level Transformation) task has no framing before it. It appears after C3 with only the task instruction — no statement of the principle behind the transformation (that narrative ordering and decision-oriented ordering produce different texts for different purposes). The B1 "Why this works" note addresses this at the model-text stage, but there is no reinforcement at the point of the C5 transformation task. A brief framing sentence before C5 would complete the teaching arc.

**Structural issue (not N10):**
H section contains an H1 Extension Task not in the profile. Profile specifies H3 only, with explicit ✅ note. The H1 should not be present.

**Verdict:** Largely passes N10. Light gap in C5 framing. Structural fix needed.
