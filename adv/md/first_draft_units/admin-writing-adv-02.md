# MODULE 1. Advanced Foundations for Administrative Writing

## Unit 2 — Tone for Agency-Level Communication

### Unit Overview

This unit develops learners’ ability to use nuanced politeness, diplomatic phrasing, and audience-sensitive tone appropriate for communication across ministries, agencies, and foreign offices. By the end of the unit, learners will be able to:

- identify tone issues in professional correspondence

- revise direct or overly strong language into neutral, diplomatic phrasing

- apply softening strategies while maintaining clarity and authority

- write audience-appropriate messages for internal and external stakeholders

### Warm-Up

#### Activity 1 — Too Direct or Appropriate?

Read each sentence and discuss whether the tone is acceptable for interagency or international communication.

1. *You did not send the correct version.*

2. *We need you to resend the file immediately.*

3. *There seems to be a discrepancy in the attached document.*

4. *Please confirm why the previous instruction was not followed.*

Identify what makes each tone effective or problematic.

#### Activity 2 — Identifying Audience

For each situation, decide whether the audience is:

- **Internal colleague (same agency)**

- **Interagency counterpart (other ministry/office)**

- **External foreign office or international organization**

Situations:

1. Requesting clarification on a translation inconsistency.

2. Informing a colleague that a procedural step was missed.

3. Asking a foreign IP office for confirmation of receipt.

4. Following up with WIPO on an outstanding inquiry.

Discuss: **How does tone change for each audience?**

### Model Text

Original version (too direct):
> *"You submitted the wrong document. Please resend the correct version. We cannot continue the review until you fix this."*

Revised version (diplomatic):
> *"It appears that the submitted document may not match the version required for this stage. To ensure accuracy in our review, we would appreciate it if you could confirm whether an updated file is available. Once received, we will be able to proceed with the next steps of the examination."*

#### Activity — Compare the Two Versions

Identify the differences in:

- tone

- level of directness

- use of softening strategies

- reader impact

### Noticing Task

Highlight examples of the following in the revised text:

1. **Softening phrases** (e.g., *it appears that*, *may not*, *would appreciate*)

2. **Reason-giving** (e.g., *to ensure accuracy*)

3. **Shared-goal framing** (e.g., *we will be able to proceed*)

4. **Removal of blame**

Discuss: How do these features change the reader’s perception?

### Language Focus

#### 1. Softening Strategies

Useful for avoiding blame, maintaining cooperation, and showing respect.

**Common forms:**

- *It appears that...*

- *There may have been an oversight regarding...*

- *It seems that...*

- *We would appreciate it if you could...*

- *Would it be possible to...*

- *To ensure accuracy...*

- *For our internal confirmation...*

**Practice A — Match the Function**
Match the softening phrase to the function:
A. *It appears that...*
B. *We would appreciate it if you could...*
C. *For our internal confirmation...*
D. *To ensure accuracy...*

Functions:

1. providing a neutral justification

2. indirect request

3. polite introduction to a possible issue

4. clarifying purpose or reason

#### 2. Neutral Clarification Language

Used for gathering information without sounding accusatory.

**Examples:**

- *Could you clarify whether...*

- *We would like to confirm the status of...*

- *For consistency, may we ask if...*

- *If available, could you share...*

**Practice B — Rewrite Direct Questions**
Rewrite the following into neutral clarification expressions:

1. *Why did you change the format?*

2. *When will you finish the revision?*

3. *Who made this decision?*

4. *Did you read the previous instructions?*

#### 3. Audience-Appropriate Tone

**Internal →** can be slightly more direct  
**Interagency →** needs neutrality and objectivity  
**International/Foreign office →** requires extra diplomacy and clarity

**Practice C — Choose the Appropriate Version**
Select the best version for each audience:

1. *"Send the corrected file today."*  

2. *"Could you kindly share the corrected file when available?"*  

3. *"We would appreciate receiving the updated file at your earliest convenience."*

### Guided Writing

#### Activity 1 — Revising the Model Text

Rewrite the original (direct) model text into a diplomatic version. Include:

- one softening phrase

- a neutral reason

#### Activity 2 — Tone Transformation

Transform each sentence into a more professional, diplomatic alternative.

1. *You didn’t follow the instructions.*

1. for an internal colleague

2. for a domestic agency

### Freer Writing

#### Task — Write a 6–8 Sentence Request Message

Choose one scenario:

1. Requesting clarification from a foreign IP office on a translation issue.

2. Asking a domestic agency for updated procedural information.

3. Following up on an outstanding confirmation required for a meeting report.

Include in your message:

- softening

- reason-giving

- a diplomatic request

- shared-goal framing

#### Peer Review

Partners identify:

- tone strengths

- overly direct expressions

- opportunities to add softening or clarification

### Editing Skills

#### Editing Checklist

Check your writing for:

- unnecessary directness

- missing softening language

- vague or implied blame

- lack of audience adaptation

- unclear purpose or reasoning

#### Editing Exercise — Reduce Directness

Rewrite these to make them more diplomatic:

1. *You did not attach the file.*

2. *This mistake caused significant delay.*

3. *We already told you to revise this.*

4. *Your clarification doesn’t make sense.*

### Extension Task

### Homework

Write a **150–170 word** diplomatic request email to a foreign office. Include:

- at least **three** softening strategies

- clear reason-giving

- a cooperative closing

- audience-appropriate tone

Ensure the message is suitable for professional interagency or international communication.
